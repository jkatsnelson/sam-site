Introduction

Car sharing is a rapidly growing sector within the larger sharing economy. It benefits two types of people: those who own cars and don’t use them often, and those who don’t own a car but want the benefits associated with car ownership.

DESIGNATION asked us to create a concept project where we imagined Uber adding carsharing as an evolution of its brand. We used our designs as explorations for how Uber might break into the sustainable carsharing space, focusing on the users who would be renting cars from others.

Our challenge was to create an app that allowed users to easily and successfully search, filter, rent, and checkout cars. These constraints gave the project scope, and allowed us to treat this project as a real project.

I worked within a team of three with UX designs that were created by a previous team. While all three teammates created their own distinct designs, we worked together to provide daily critique and support to each other, as well as prepared weekly presentations documenting our progress.

Digging Into the UX

The UX team before us conducted a plethora of UX work to help guide our UI designs, and upon starting they gave us all of this work, including personas and wireframes.

Persona

Erin, 30
  • Working professional living in San Francisco
  • Looking for convenience as well as reliability
  • Struggles juggling the demands of her busy life, needs the freedom of car ownership without the additional stress of ownership

We were initially given two very different personas, and for the purposes and scope of this concept project I focused my designs on the needs of one persona, “Erin”. She is someone who I felt was a better business fit for our fictional Uber spin-off, and would become a regular user of the app.

Mid-fidelity Wireframes

We found a redundancy within the wireframes that made for clunky UX: there were two tab bar items that essentially performed the same function. After consulting with the UX team, we combined these two items before moving forward to learn more about other companies in the car sharing space.

Exploring the Car
Sharing Space

After fixing the UX, we analyzed nine companies in the market, from business-to-consumer (B2C) to peer-to-peer (P2P) companies. Enterprise and Turo are on two ends of this spectrum, and I used what I learned from both to help inform my designs.

Enterprise is a well-known and B2C. Its designs feel corporate and dull with washed-out greys, unmemorable typography, and UX that’s clunky and lacking in innovation.
Turo is new and P2P. It has edgy designs that are fresh and new, using bright purple, a retro serif font, and dark UI. But its not as trustworthy due to its newness in the space.

Design Principles

After diving into the UX and learning more about the existing car sharing space, we created design principles to guide our future designs and process.

Make it reliable, use white space
At its core, Spore’s purpose is to serve as a utilitarian app to automate mushroom production. We speak to this purpose through a sensitivity to clean, understated layouts of visual elements and data.

familiar, & relaxed
Spore creates a direct and simple experience with its users by drawing upon the resourceful combination of white space, a mindful application of color, and an intuitive set of icons. Each makes it easy for the user to find the information they need, when they need it or simply watch their mushrooms grow.

Creative Exploration

The Name

After much ideation, we came up with the name UberGo. We kept Uber in the title to “Go” encompassed the feelings the brand needed: the freedom of having a car without owning one, the spontaneity that comes with having on-demand access to a car, and the openness to going wherever, whenever with the app.

Iterations & Testing

We conducted multiple rounds of usability testing with users including Roman, Todd, and Spore’s developer, Justin. While Roman was our main user, we also leaned on the others’ feedback, especially Justin’s, in order to shape our designs and help guide Spore’s UI.

Iteration 1

Previous Next

I used blue as my primary color to distinguish Spore from the other brands which tended to use light greens. Overall, users said the designs felt too clean, minimal, and stark. They didn’t like the red or the rectangular buttons, but liked the typography and overall layout. The users were also interested in seeing a color other than blue as the call to action.

I used this feedback to iteration on my designs for the second round of testing.

Iteration 2

Previous Next

Users liked the rounded buttons and the overall softer approach to the design, and found the graph, icons, and overall layout clear and simple. Most users liked intoducing teal; Roman did not. Users also liked the idea of color-coding alerts for easy scannability, but didn’t feel that the chosen colors were the right ones.

Finding the Right
Shade of Blue

Earlier, Roman said that he was open to colors other than blue, but after the second round of testing he changed his mind and stated he only wanted shades of blue. Because Roman continued to go back and forth about Spore’s color, we prepared an exercise to help find the perfect palette.

We had Roman and Todd choose color swatches for the app, and then come together and agree upon colors. During this exercise Todd relinquished control of color choosing to Roman, who explained that he wanted a palette of blues because he needed the design to be easy for him to build upon. To him, blues felt modern, approachable, flexible, and more of a tech product and less of a plant-matter product.

During this exercise we reached a compromise with Roman to bring in other colors for alerts. We stressed the importance of making the app easy to scan, especially for everyday users. We came away from the exercise with a general palette: monochromatic blue with yellow, green, and red for different kinds of alerts.

Final Designs

After three rounds of usability testing with our users, four meetings, the color exercise, and over six iterations, I crafted my final designs. Throughout the process, I kept our design principles and future users in mind, keeping the designs clean, scannable, effective, and familiar.

Style Guide

After chatting with Justin, Spore’s developer, about his preferred handoff methods, we decided to use Zeplin to make development quick and easy. Additionally, I created a style guide that included colors, brand attributes, UI elements, and design patterns to aid future designers working on Spore.

Future Considerations

Previous Next

At the end of our time designing Spore’s UI, we documented future considerations for Spore. We recommended that future versions consider the needs of different users, such as those using Spore for commercial production versus at-home production.

Roman said Spore’s ultimate future was anything climate-controlled, not just mushrooms. Because of this ultimate goal, we recommended beginning to think about the UX and corresponding UI for different products that would go inside Spore, from mushrooms to cheese to meat.

We suggested incorporating images and links to educational content for novice users, to begin to consider where to store all the notes, to reconsider the “Scheduled Events” UX, and to incorporate haptic feedback into the app to make it especially easier for power users.

Conclusion

I found designing Spore challenging and rewarding. I learned how to display data in many different ways, and thought deeply about the importance of hierarchy to effectively show information and guide the user. The UI design was shaped by ever-changing UX, client, and user needs, and I needed to think on my feet and be flexible enough to quickly switch gears, redesign directions, and continually test. This helped me become a better designer, and for that I am thankful.

I learned about working directly with clients, and things to do differently in the future. The color exercise was extremely helpful in pinning down colors to use and getting our client to think deeply and carefully about the app’s color. I wish we had conducted this exercise earlier in our process, and it’s something I will incorporate into future kickoff meetings.

I initially found it difficult to work with a product that was evolving every day, but learned how to work in shorter bursts for shorter feedback loops. During my time designing Spore, I kept in constant contact with my client in order to be on top of changes and even begin to anticipate them.

Ultimately I was both happy and proud of the work I produced: I iterated continuously to create a design that fit the needs of the UX, the client, and the users, and grew as a designer along the way.
