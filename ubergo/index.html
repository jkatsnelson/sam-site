Introduction

Car sharing is a rapidly growing sector within the larger sharing economy. It benefits two types of people: those who own cars and don’t use them often, and those who don’t own a car but want the benefits associated with car ownership.

DESIGNATION asked us to create a concept project where we imagined Uber adding carsharing as an evolution of its brand. We used our designs as explorations for how Uber might break into the sustainable carsharing space, focusing on the users who would be renting cars from others.

Our challenge was to create an app that allowed users to easily and successfully search, filter, rent, and checkout cars. These constraints gave the project scope, and allowed us to treat this project as a real project.

I worked within a team of three with UX designs that were created by a previous team. While all three teammates created their own distinct designs, we worked together to provide daily critique and support to each other, as well as prepared weekly presentations documenting our progress.

Digging Into the UX

The UX team before us conducted a plethora of UX work to help guide our UI designs, and upon starting they gave us all of this work, including personas and wireframes.

Persona

Erin, 30
  • Working professional living in San Francisco
  • Looking for convenience as well as reliability
  • Struggles juggling the demands of her busy life, needs the freedom of car ownership without the additional stress of ownership

We were initially given two very different personas, and for the purposes and scope of this concept project I focused my designs on the needs of one persona, “Erin”. She is someone who I felt was a better business fit for our fictional Uber spin-off, and would become a regular user of the app.

Mid-fidelity Wireframes

We found a redundancy within the wireframes that made for clunky UX: there were two tab bar items that essentially performed the same function. After consulting with the UX team, we combined these two items before moving forward to learn more about other companies in the car sharing space.

Exploring the Car
Sharing Space

After fixing the UX, we analyzed nine companies in the market, from business-to-consumer (B2C) to peer-to-peer (P2P) companies. Enterprise and Turo are on two ends of this spectrum, and I used what I learned from both to help inform my designs.

Enterprise is a well-known and B2C. Its designs feel corporate and dull with washed-out greys, unmemorable typography, and UX that’s clunky and lacking in innovation.
Turo is new and P2P. It has edgy designs that are fresh and new, using bright purple, a retro serif font, and dark UI. But its not as trustworthy due to its newness in the space.

Design Principles

After diving into the UX and learning more about the existing car sharing space, we created design principles to guide our future designs and process.

Make it reliable,
use white space
Our designs are the perfect combination of new and reliable: you can trust the Uber brand to get you from point A to B. Through use of ample white space and guiding colors, the designs are pleasing to the eye and easy to navigate.
Make it easy,
give freedom
Car sharing gives those without cars freedom to run errands on their own time, in peace. The designs add to this enjoyability through friendly and colloquial language and clear next steps.

Make car
sharing enjoyable
Our designs show we care about our users through use of friendly photography and trustworthy car imagery. Through clean UI, the app makes it easy to find, rent, and review cars.

Creative Exploration

The Name

After much ideation, we came up with the name UberGo. We kept Uber in the title to “Go” encompassed the feelings the brand needed: the freedom of having a car without owning one, the spontaneity that comes with having on-demand access to a car, and the openness to going wherever, whenever with the app.

Logos

I explored many directions for UberGo’s logo, and worked through ideas of cars and more abstract designs to further the feeling UberGo’s design. As I worked, I kept the needs of Erin and our design principles in mind.

Previous Next

Ultimately I decided to go with the abstract blue triangle design. The points alluded to a compass and the freedom of having access to a car; the serif font differentiated UberGo from the other competitors, and offered nostaligia from a world of self-driving cars. I felt this logo aligned most with Erin’s needs and personality, and was a combination of clean, modern, and fun.

Style Exploration

I explored three directions for UberGo’s design, and worked through designs that spoke to different aspects of Erin’s personality and needs.

Style Tile: Contemporary
Style Tile: Spirited
Style Tile: Hip
Previous Next

The “Contemporary” style tile conveyed the look and feel I wanted UberGo to portray. There’s plenty of whitespace, crisp colors to guide the user, and sophisticated typography to appeal to Erin. I paired thin, linear icons with bright bold colors and crisp car imagery to appeal to Erin, someone who liked both the fun and practicality of driving. I decided to pull layouts from the other two style tiles before I moved onto fleshing out the app’s final design.

Final Designs

Mobile App

I arrived at my final designs through multiple rounds of iterations. My designs didn’t have any major pivot points, but I continually worked to hone the design. I strove to design the layout and flows to ensure they were best for Erin’s needs, the app’s primary use cases, and our design principles.

I also dug into how to use the UI to inform different use cases, which brought me to my final designs.

Previous Next

Responsive Design

As supplements to the app, I created a responsive landing site for the app. I kept the palette and the clean, simple layout the same as is the UberGo app. I wrote the copy for the designs to further aid in the site’s experience, and kept it light and informative.

Style Guide

Although this was a concept project, I created a style guide to further outline the technical feasibility of my designs and think about how developers would implement them. My style guide helped me streamline my design to ensure consistency in color, layout, typography, and font size.

Future Considerations

Previous Next

I was interested in UberGo as a living product, and I considered what it would look like in the future. Future versions would also have a “rentee” side, where those who were renting out their cars would switch to the other side of the app. Over time the app would learn from the user, thus introducing new expanded designs that take this into consideration.

Conclusion

Overall, I was proud of my designs and what they (and I!) accomplished. The user flow is simple and intuitive, the colors guide the user through the tasks, and the simplified car views and checkout process ensure user enjoyment and success.

I learned how to work quickly and lean on others for feedback and advice to help evolve my designs. Within my team, I prepared weekly presentations, which helped me grow in confidence and skill. I learned how to explain and defend my design decisions, and honed my presentation skills, something I found invaluable to the design process. I learned to dive into user’s needs and goals, and how to ask pointed questions from others to help my designs meet these needs.

Working on this project on a team set me up for success on my following projects, where I worked with real clients to design their products. I brought what I learned during UberGo to drive my future designs’ successes, and especially leaned on the importance of strong UX, continual feedback and iteration, and user-driven design.
